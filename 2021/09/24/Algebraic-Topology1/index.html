Chapter 0

1.

It is not easy to construct a explicit deformation retraction directly on the torus. However, if we notice that a torus is homeomorphism to a square with each pair of opposite edges glued respectively, then the representation would be much easier.

In that perspective, a torus with one point deleted is homeomorphism to the quotient space of a square with one point deleted, and the desired image space induces the very four edges of the square.

The deformation retraction from a "glued square with one point deleted" to its edges is obvious to construct.

3.

We prove (b) first.

Suppose \(f,g,h:X\to Y\). Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are in the homotopy \(F(x,t)\) with \(f(x)=F(x,t_1), g(x)=F(x,t_2)\), and \(g\) and \(h\) are in the homotopy \(G(x,t)\) with \(g(x)=G(x,t_3), h(x)=G(x,t_4)\).

Then we notice that \(H(x,t)=\begin{cases}F(x,t_1)&t<t_1\\G(x,t+t_3-t_1)&t\ge t_1\end{cases}\) is a homotopy contains \(f,g,h\), i. e. \(f\) and \(h\) are homotopic.

Suppose \(f_1:X\to Y\) and \(f_2:Y\to Z\) are homotopy equivalence respectively, i. e. there exists \(g_1:Y\to X\) s. t. \(f_1\circ g_1\simeq\mathrm{ide}_Y\) and \(g_1\circ f_1\simeq\mathrm{ide}_X\), \(g_2:Z\to Y\) s. t. \(f_2\circ g_2\simeq\mathrm{ide}_z\) and \(g_2\circ f_2\simeq\mathrm{ide}_Y\).

We are going to show that \(g_1\circ g_2\circ f_2\circ f_1\simeq\mathrm{ide}_X\) and \(f_2\circ f_1\circ g_1\circ g_2\simeq\mathrm{ide}_Z\), which by definition deduce that \(X\) is homotopy equivalent to \(Z\).

Take \(g_1\circ g_2\circ f_2\circ f_1\) as example. Since \(g_1\circ f_1\simeq\mathrm{ide}_X\) and in (b) above we have proved that relation of homotopy among maps is an equivalence relation, we only need to prove \(g_1\circ g_2\circ f_2\circ f_1\simeq g_1\circ f_1\).

Since \(g_2\circ f_2\simeq\mathrm{ide}_Y\), there exists homotopy \(F(y,t)\) s. t. \(F(y_,0)=g_2\circ f_2,F(y,1)=\mathrm{ide}_Y\). Then we know that \(G(x,t)=g_1(x)\circ F(y,t)\circ f_1(x)\) is a homotopy with \(G(x_,0)=g_1\circ g_2\circ f_2\circ f_1,G(x,1)=g_1\circ f_1\), i. e. \(g_1\circ g_2\circ f_2\circ f_1\simeq g_1\circ f_1\simeq\mathrm{ide}_X\).

\(f_2\circ f_1\circ g_1\circ g_2\simeq\mathrm{ide}_Z\) is of the same reason.

It is easy to show that if \(f\simeq g\), then \(h\circ f\simeq h\circ g\) and \(f\circ h\simeq g\circ h\).

4.

Denote the inclusion \(A\to X\) by \(i\). We are going to prove that \(i\circ f_1\simeq\mathrm{ide}_X\) and \(f_1\circ i\simeq\mathrm{ide}_A\).

\(i\circ f_1\simeq\mathrm{ide}_X\) is obvious since \(i\circ f_1=f_1\) and \(f_1\simeq\mathrm{ide}_X\) due to the weak deformation retraction.

For \(f_1\circ i\simeq\mathrm{ide}_A\), notice that \(G(x,t)=f_t|_A\) which is a homotopy with \(G(x,0)=\mathrm{ide}_A\) and \(G(x,1)=f_1\circ i\).

5.

As the deformation retraction gives us the homotopy \(F(x,t)=f_t(x)\), it is natural to consider \(F|_V\) as the required homotopy connects the inclusion \(V\hookrightarrow U\) and the constant map \(V\to x\) . What we need to prove is that, for any given \(U\), there exists \(V\subset U\) s. t. \(f_t(V)\subset U\) for all \(t\in[0,1]\).

Since \(f_t(x)=x\in U\) for all \(t\), by continuity, for each \(t\) in \(I=[0,1]\) there is a neighborhood \(W(t)\) of \(t\) in \(I\) and a neighborhood \(V(t)\) of \(x\) in \(X\) such that \(F( V(t) \times W(t) )\) is contained in \(U\). Since the \(W(t)\) form a covering of \(I\) and \(I\) is compact, finitely many of the \(W(t)\) form a covering of \(I\) due to the Heine–Borel theorem. Let \(V\) be the intersection of \(U\) with all the corresponding finitely many \(V(t)\). Then \(V\) is a neighborhood of \(x\) contained in \(U\) such that \(F(V \times I)\) is contained in \(U\).

Otherwise. We will use the conclusion in problem 5. to make contradiction. If the space deformation retracts to a point \((x,y)\) with \(y\neq0\), then for any neighborhood \(U\ni (x,y)\), there exists a neighborhood \(V\subset U\) s. t. the inclusion \(i:V\hookrightarrow U\) is nullhomotopic. However, we know that any neighborhood \(V\) of \((x,y)\) is not connected, in particular, not path-connected.

Remark that a space is impossible to be contractible if it is not path-connected. Suppose the space \(X\) can contract to an point \(x_0\), then for each point \(x\) there is a continuous map \(f_{x}(t)\) from \(I=[0,1]\) to \(X\) s. t. \(f(0)=x\) and \(f(1)=x_0\). Then for each pair of points \(x_1,x_2\), the composition of its \(f\) induces a new continuous map from \(I\) to \(X\) s. t. \(f(0)=x_1\) and \(f(1)=x_2\), which induce a path connects \(x_1\) and \(x_2\).

For the same reason, the inclusion \(i:V\hookrightarrow U\) can not be nullhomotopic.

  1. & (c)

We will directly give the method that deformation retracts \(Y\) to \(Z\) in the weak sense.

The key thing is to slide the base as well! Simply move every point in \(Z\) along the zigzag to the right at a constant speed of contracting the triangles. As points in the "bristles" reach the base, have them turn the corner and start sliding along the zigzag as well, and as points reach the end of one base have them turn the corner on the zigzag and continue to the right at the same speed.

Remark that in the method above we contracting the triangles at a constant speed but not proportional as we did in (a). That is, the point in the short bristles will reach the base first and then slide along the zigzag, while the points in the long bristles are still contracting. When the point of each longest bristles \(\{0\}\times[0,1]\) reaches \((0,0)\), the deformation retraction is over.

11.

Notice that \(g\simeq(h\circ f)\circ g=h\circ (f\circ g)\simeq h\), thus \(g\circ f\simeq h\circ f\simeq\text{ide}_Y\)。

Since \(f\circ g\) and \(h\circ f\) is homotopy equivalence respectively, there exists \(a:Y\to Y\) s. t. \(f\circ g\circ a\simeq\text{ide}_Y\) and \(b: X\to X\) s. t. \(\text{ide}_X\simeq b\circ h\circ f\). Thus from the conclusion in (i) we know that \(f\) is a homotopy equivalence between \(X\) and \(Y\).

P. S. We remark that in the \(f:X\to X\) situation, "\(f\simeq\text{ide}_X\)" is stronger than "\(f\) is a homotopy equivalence of \(X\) itself".
